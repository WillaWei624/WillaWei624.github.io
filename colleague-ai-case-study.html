Context

An AI grading platform that teachers couldn't navigate alone

Colleague AI is a K-12 ed-tech platform helping educators create assessments, build rubrics with AI, and use an AI grader to generate student feedback. The company's goal: reduce training time required to use the platform. They needed to understand where and why teachers were getting stuck.

Our team was contracted to evaluate the Classroom feature — specifically three core teacher workflows — with active 6th–12th grade teachers across Washington State.

Research design

A rigorous three-phase moderated study

Sessions were conducted remotely via Zoom, approximately 60 minutes each. We recruited active 6th–12th grade teachers currently working in Washington State public schools. All participants were active Canvas users — giving us a strong mental model baseline for comparison against Colleague AI's interface.

Pre-test interview
Established mental model baseline before participants touched the platform
🖥️
Think-aloud tasks

My process

How I contributed across the full study

1
Planning
Study design & participant recruitment
Co-developed research questions, task scenarios, and screener criteria. Recruited 6th–12th grade teachers across Washington State via district websites and Calendly. Contributed to the informed consent form and moderator script.
2
Data collection
Moderated sessions with rotating team roles
Rotated across moderator, timer, and logger roles using Critical Incident Technique (CIT) forms to capture breakdown and satisfaction moments in real time across all 5 sessions.
3
Analysis
Triangulation: said · did · rated
Cross-referenced qualitative think-aloud data, behavioral observations, and quantitative SEQ scores. Classified issues by severity (Low / Medium / High) using Jeff Sauro's framework, with frequency counts across participants.
4
Deliverable
40-page professional research report
Structured report covering executive summary, research questions, participant profiles, methodology, per-feature findings, prioritized recommendations, reflections, and future explorations. Delivered to Colleague AI's team.

Reflection

What real client research taught me

1
Test the platform yourself before testing with participants. Our team missed a critical feature during task design — we only discovered its importance when every participant encountered the same failure point. With real clients, preparation gaps become data gaps.
2
Prior tool habits are data, not noise. Every participant was a Canvas user and their expectations were calibrated to that system. When designing AI-powered workflows, you're competing with deeply ingrained patterns — acknowledging this is the only way to design past it.
3
Domain context shapes what you find. One participant's frustration revealed a gap we hadn't anticipated — specific to their subject area. Deeper background screening before sessions would have helped us design for it. The richest insights often come from unexpected places.
