TRU Wellness — where recovery meets campus life

Designed "Harmony" biophilic wellness pods for UW students — combining physical space design with a full digital booking app and in-pod kiosk interface. A 28-page design specification delivered to campus stakeholders.

UX/UI Designer & Researcher

Scope
Research · Physical Design · App · Kiosk UI
The problem

Students burn out in the gaps between classes

UW students frequently have 30–60 minute breaks between classes, yet campus spaces — hallways, crowded cafés, libraries — offer no real support for mental recovery. Stress peaks mid-week (66.7% of surveyed students), but existing wellness programs are appointment-based, time-intensive, or socially stigmatized.

The challenge was designing a solution that fits into students' actual rhythms: quick, private, on-demand, and accessible without stigma.

"How can we design wellness spaces to address student stress management?"

— Our core design question
Research

Online surveys + semi-structured interviews

We conducted online surveys collecting quantitative data on stress drivers, times, locations, coping behaviors, and willingness to try new wellness solutions — alongside 20–30 minute semi-structured interviews to understand context and emotional needs.

Four key insights emerged from synthesis: students prioritize proximity and low effort above everything; they want 10–45 minute private micro-breaks with sensory control; there is strong need for a private personal space (praying, pumping, crying); and existing wellness resources are simply too far away to use between classes.

Design process

From cardboard mockup to Figma prototype

1
Ideation
Concept generation — three directions
We explored biophilic pod design, artificial sunlight therapy, and flexible privacy spaces. Research data pointed us clearly toward a multi-mode enclosed pod that supports diverse recovery needs without stigma.
2
Physical design
Cardboard architectural mockup (Wizard of Oz)
Built a life-size cardboard pod with adjustable lamp, kiosk mockup, and SOS/emergency signage. Three participants navigated the space as "Stressed Sam," "Sleepy Sarah," and "Exercising Eli" — validating spatial comfort, sensory balance, and layout before any digital investment.
3
Digital design
Booking app + in-pod kiosk interface
Designed two separate digital experiences in Figma: a mobile booking app (location → time → pod → payment) and a kiosk/tablet interface inside the pod (massage chair, music, greenery room, digital detox box, light modes, accessibility controls). Both support multilingual switching, voice assist, and emergency access.
4
Testing
Usability testing — physical + digital
Scenario-based walkthroughs (imagine you have 45 minutes after a draining class), think-aloud protocol, and post-session interviews on perceived value, willingness to pay, and hygiene concerns. Key finding: replace "biophilic" with "nature-inspired" in all user-facing copy.
5
Deliverable
28-page design specification
Delivered a comprehensive blueprint covering problem definition, research synthesis, personas, user flows, design rationale matrix, physical pod specs, digital interface specs, privacy considerations, and future commercialization strategy.
Design solution

Harmony Wellness Pods — physical + digital

Physical Space
Biophilic pod exterior & interior
Clean, minimal, fully enclosed pods with integrated greenery placed in high-traffic campus areas. Interior features a massage/nap chair, artificial sunlamp, digital detox lockbox, projector screen for immersive nature scenes, and scent options — all controllable via the kiosk tablet.
Mobile App
Location → time → pod → payment
A 5-step booking flow where students find nearby pods sorted by distance, select 30-minute time slots ($5 each, max 2 per day), choose a specific pod by location within the building, pay via credit card / Apple Pay / Husky Card, and receive a confirmation with pod access code.
Kiosk / Tablet Interface
Full pod control from one screen
Six main modes: Massage Chair (soft/medium/strong, lay flat, zero gravity), Music (curated playlists + Bluetooth), Greenery Room (immersive nature scenes: Rainforest, Ocean, Waterfall…), Digital Detox Box, Light Mode (stimulate sunlight vs normal), and Accessibility Options (voice assist, multilingual, emergency).
Key design decisions

What the research changed

Usability testing directly shaped four design decisions that we might not have made otherwise:

1
Language matters. "Biophilic design" confused participants — replaced with "nature-inspired wellness space" in all user-facing text. Technical vocabulary was a real barrier to adoption.
2
Phone rules need nuance. A blanket "no tech" policy failed — students need their phones for audio pass-through and accessibility. Switched to mode-based rules instead.
3
Massage chair is the center. Testing confirmed the chair as the most valued feature — placed it at the spatial and experiential center of the pod, with intuitive time presets.
4
Trust through transparency. Hygiene concerns were high — addressed by showing pod inventory and cleaning practices in the booking interface, with explicit pricing visible before commitment.
